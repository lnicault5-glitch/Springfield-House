Springfield in 1948

In his 1948 travel book Islands to Windward, sailor and writer Carlton Mitchell described Springfield as a lively gathering place. At that time, the estate was a modest plantation house above the Roseau Valley, not yet transformed into the larger residence and guest facilities Archbold would later build.

Mitchell recalled sailors, travelers, and locals climbing the hill to Springfield to share in John’s hospitality. On Saturday evenings the estate filled with music:

“The band assembled on the terrace after dinner. The melody was carried by a lone flutist, joined by drums stretched from wooden tubs, a triangle struck with a valve stem, guitars, a hollow bamboo ‘boom-boom,’ and a rattling tin shak-shak. The rhythm was irresistible — heads bobbed, hands clapped, and laughter carried into the night.”

This scene shows Springfield’s early role as a social and cultural crossroads — a place of music, fellowship, and welcome that laid the foundation for its later chapters in science and philanthropy.

The Story of Springfield

The Springfield property has deep roots in Dominican history. Originally owned by the Bellot family, the land was later purchased in 1935 by John Dana Archbold, whose vision and philanthropy shaped much of its legacy. In the 1940s, Archbold constructed Springfield House, now preserved as a museum.

In the world that greeted Archbold, Dominica’s valleys were dense with fern and cocoa, the air alive with birdsong and the scent of rain. Writers like Rosita Forbes spoke of it as an “island in the sun,” a name that still feels true today.

Beside the main residence, Mr Archbold built the Springfield Guest House, which for many years served as one of Dominica’s most sought-after places to stay. Known for its classic atmosphere and old-world charm, the Guest House was a lively hub of community life, even hosting weddings and social gatherings. Following his passion for education and development, John Dana Archbold contributed to the creation of the Archbold Tropical Research and Education Center. The main house, however, remained private — until now.

Today, Springfield House is being carefully restored and opened to the public for the first time. Visitors can view the preserved rooms, antique furnishings, art collections, and the surrounding gardens, while also enjoying a dramatic viewpoint of the Caribbean Sea over what was once a banana and grapefruit plantation.

Together, Springfield House and the Guest House are cultural landmarks whose preservation is in the national interest. They embody Dominica’s resilience, community spirit, and the intertwining of personal stories with the island’s wider history.

Furnishings with a Story

Mr Archbold brought and collected antique furniture — some imported, some commissioned, and many purchased from other estates. From South Chilton Estate (through figures such as Mr. Henderson) to acquisitions from Maggie Nicholls of Kingsland House, each piece carries a thread of Dominican history. As Kingsland House itself had belonged to Dr. John Imray (1811–1880), a renowned Scottish scientist, some furniture may well trace its origins back to Europe, reflecting both local and international heritage.

The Art Collection

Perhaps the most remarkable treasure of Springfield House is its art collection. Alongside a few older prints, Archbold assembled numerous oil paintings, most created during the 1980s and 1990s by Dominican artists. Among them are early works by internationally acclaimed painter Earl Etienne, as well as pieces by Darius, Louison, K. Royer, and others. These works depict traditional life, community scenes, and the island’s landscapes.

Together, these paintings form a vivid portrait of Dominica’s culture and memory, adding depth to the museum experience and confirming Springfield’s role as a guardian of national heritage.

These collections make Springfield more than a home — they turn it into a living museum, where each object is part of a broader story of resilience, exchange, and cultural memory.

People of Springfield

The story of Springfield also belongs to its people. Cecilia, Archbold’s personal chef, cared for him in the last years of his life. Desmond Eusell, born on the property, still tends the grounds today with his sons. Mr. Bruno, once manager of Springfield, was able to purchase land in Sylvania and build a family home thanks to his work here. Mrs. Alexander celebrated her wedding at Springfield in 1998, continuing the tradition of the house as a place of family, community, and cherished memories.

Their lives and celebrations are part of Springfield’s legacy of opportunity, resilience, and belonging — proof that the house is not only a historic landmark, but a living part of Dominican life.

Springfield’s story unfolds across decades — a journey of vision, resilience, and renewal.

1935

Mr Archbold purchases the Springfield property from the Bellot family.

1940s

Springfield House built by Mr Archbold, furnished with antiques from across Dominica and beyond.

1970s–2017

The house becomes a social hub: weddings, music events, and community employment flourish.

1989

Mr Archbold donates a large portion of the property to create the Archbold Tropical Research Center, welcoming universities to conduct tropical research.

2017 – Hurricane Maria

Hurricane Maria devastates Dominica, damaging Springfield but marking a new chapter of resilience and renewal.

2022 – Restoration with Purpose

Community-led restoration begins, reopening Springfield House as a museum and nature walk for all.
